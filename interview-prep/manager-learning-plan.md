# Manager Learning Plan

## Goal
Build a practical, reusable curriculum for learning how to be an effective engineering manager, with enough structure to:
- study in the right dependency order
- connect frameworks to real work situations
- turn lessons into interview-ready stories
- keep adding new questions without losing focus

## How We Will Use This
For each module, we will work through:
1. the core framework
2. concrete examples
3. common mistakes and failure modes
4. how to apply it in real work
5. how to talk about it in interviews

## Core Principle
We are not going to study topics in the order they appeared originally. We will study them in dependency order, so each topic makes the next one easier.

## Learning Sequence

### 1. Role, Scope, and Leverage
Understand what a manager actually owns, what authority is optional, and how to drive change without waiting for formal power.

Topics:
- what a manager is responsible for
- what "working out of your job" means
- how to create leverage without authority
- what to do when you see a problem but do not control the org chart, budget, or policy
- when to escalate vs influence vs decide
- what actions you can initiate even if you cannot fire, promote, or reorg people directly

Why first:
Most of the other questions become easier once role boundaries and leverage are clear.

### 2. Communication and Alignment
Learn how to communicate upward, sideways, and downward so work stays aligned and trust stays high.

Topics:
- how to advocate to management
- communication with stakeholders
- managing expectations early
- how different companies handle conflict
- how to identify the conflict style that fits a specific company
- how to disagree without becoming oppositional

Why second:
Management is largely a communication job. Planning and execution fail quickly without alignment.

### 3. Planning, Prioritization, and Delivery
Learn how to convert goals into plans across multiple time horizons and manage risk before delivery slips.

Topics:
- planning across short-, medium-, and long-term timeframes
- navigating ambiguity
- prioritization
- hard tradeoffs
- how to avoid high-risk rewrites driven by team excitement
- how to handle cases where you could not deliver on time

Why third:
This is where management starts becoming operational rather than theoretical.

### 4. Team Execution and Delegation
Learn how to keep work moving through other people rather than by doing everything yourself.

Topics:
- how to help your team get unstuck
- delegation frameworks
- what to do when delegated work misses deadline or quality bar
- how to delegate when there is very little schedule or budget buffer
- ways to improve developer experience
- framework to find usability or process issues in the team's work

Why fourth:
This is the practical bridge between planning and people management.

### 5. 1:1s, Feedback, and Coaching
Learn the operating mechanics of managing individuals well.

Topics:
- approach to 1:1s
- repeatable 1:1 frameworks
- specific examples of effective 1:1 conversations
- constructive feedback
- coaching for growth, ownership, and accountability

Why fifth:
Once the operating model is clear, 1:1s become a tool for execution and development, not just conversation.

### 6. Performance, Conflict, and Inherited Teams
Learn how to handle the uncomfortable cases that distinguish real management from lightweight coordination.

Topics:
- low performer cases
- conflict cases
- arrogant developer cases
- how to handle an underperforming team you inherited
- how to take responsibility for a team you did not form
- what accountability looks like when estimates are team-generated but delivery is weak

Why sixth:
These topics depend on understanding leverage, communication, delivery, delegation, and coaching first.

### 7. Hiring and Team Design
Learn when and how to add people, and what part of hiring is actually the manager's job.

Topics:
- how to hire
- what hiring managers should know
- what HR owns vs what the manager owns
- sourcing through LinkedIn and personal networks
- evaluating candidates
- hiring stories
- team formation and rotations

Why seventh:
Hiring matters, but it is easier to learn once the basics of running a team are already grounded.

### 8. Metrics and Management Operating System
Learn how to measure whether the team is healthy and whether management actions are working.

Topics:
- metrics
- what to measure and what not to measure
- delivery metrics
- quality metrics
- team health metrics
- leading vs lagging indicators

Why eighth:
Metrics make sense only after we understand the behaviors and systems they are meant to observe.

### 9. Story Bank and Interview Translation
Turn real management situations into clear narratives for interviews and reflection.

Stories to build:
- low performer
- conflict
- hiring
- prioritization
- hard tradeoffs
- ambiguity
- arrogant developer
- constructive feedback
- mistake

Why ninth:
Stories are strongest when built on top of the full management model instead of as isolated anecdotes.

## Module 1 Notes: Role, Scope, and Leverage

### Objective
Build a clear model for what a manager is responsible for, what kinds of power matter, and how to make progress even when formal authority is limited.

### Core Idea
A manager's job is not mainly to do more work personally. It is to create the conditions for a team to make good decisions, execute reliably, and improve over time.

### What a Manager Actually Owns
- team outcomes, not just team activity
- clarity of priorities
- risk visibility
- execution health
- team operating environment
- talent assessment and development
- escalation when the team cannot solve a problem locally

### What a Manager Usually Does Not Fully Control
- compensation decisions
- headcount approval
- final HR process
- organization design above the team level
- cross-functional dependencies
- company-wide policy

### Important Principle
You can be accountable for an outcome without having unilateral authority over every input.

### Leverage Model
When you want to change something, identify which of these levers are available:

1. Direct control
You can decide and implement it inside your team's scope.

2. Prioritization
You can change what gets attention, sequencing, staffing, or time.

3. Process
You can change reviews, planning rituals, quality bars, handoffs, or checklists.

4. Information
You can expose hidden risk, create visibility, or frame the problem more clearly.

5. Relationships
You can build support from peers, partner teams, senior engineers, recruiting, product, or leadership.

6. Escalation
You can raise a decision when impact is material and local influence is insufficient.

### Practical Decision Rule
When you see a problem:
1. define the problem in business terms
2. identify what is within team scope
3. choose the smallest lever that can realistically move it
4. create visibility early if risk is growing
5. escalate only after you can explain the tradeoff clearly

### Working Out of Your Job
Healthy version:
- you proactively solve problems adjacent to your role
- you create reusable improvements, not heroics
- you make ownership clearer over time

Unhealthy version:
- you absorb work that should stay delegated
- you compensate for broken systems indefinitely
- you become the bottleneck while calling it ownership

### Signs You Have More Leverage Than You Think
- you can change team rituals
- you can raise standards
- you can redefine what "done" means
- you can surface tradeoffs earlier
- you can bring decisions to the correct forum
- you can shape hiring signals even if you do not own recruiting operations

### Common Mistakes
- waiting for formal authority before acting
- escalating too early without a clear problem statement
- owning outcomes by taking tasks back personally
- treating lack of budget as lack of agency
- confusing influence with politics in a negative sense

### Example Scenarios

#### Scenario: You cannot approve headcount, but the team is overloaded
Manager move:
Show the tradeoff explicitly. Reduce scope, sequence work, or quantify the cost of not hiring so leadership makes a real decision instead of absorbing silent failure.

#### Scenario: You cannot fire someone, but performance is poor
Manager move:
Document expectations, give feedback, create a support plan, track progress, and partner with HR or your manager for formal process when needed.

#### Scenario: You think a rewrite is dangerous, but the team is excited
Manager move:
Change the decision frame from enthusiasm to risk, opportunity cost, reversibility, and milestone-based proof.

### First Practical Question for Future Discussion
For any problem you bring, we should ask:
"What do I actually control, what can I influence, and what must I escalate?"

### Influence Without Authority
Influence without authority is not vague charisma. It is usually built from a repeatable set of moves:

1. Clarify the decision
Many conflicts are not really conflicts. They are unresolved questions about owner, scope, tradeoff, or timing.

2. Translate the problem
Frame it in terms the other party cares about:
- leadership: business risk, delivery confidence, cost, visibility
- engineers: technical risk, maintenance burden, reversibility
- product: customer impact, time-to-learn, scope tradeoffs
- HR or recruiting: process, fairness, throughput, candidate quality

3. Reduce the ask
Influence is easier when you ask for a smaller, testable decision instead of a sweeping commitment.

4. Bring evidence
Use examples, trend data, missed commitments, incident patterns, customer pain, or cost of delay.

5. Bring options
Do not escalate a problem as a complaint. Escalate it as a decision with tradeoffs.

### Escalation Quality
Good escalation includes:
- what happened
- why it matters
- what has already been tried
- available options
- recommendation
- consequence of inaction

Weak escalation sounds like:
- "the team is blocked"
- "people are not aligned"
- "we need help"

Strong escalation sounds like:
- "We can ship on time only if we cut scope X, or we can keep scope and accept a four-week delay. We have already reduced Y and Z. I recommend cutting X because the missed date will affect dependency A."

### When to Escalate
Escalate when:
- a decision crosses team boundaries
- the tradeoff affects company priorities, not just local preferences
- you need authority you do not have
- risk is material and time-sensitive
- local attempts have failed

Do not escalate just because:
- the conversation is uncomfortable
- you disagree but have not framed the tradeoff well
- you want someone senior to own the tension for you

### Avoiding Hero Mode
Managers often destroy leverage by stepping in as the smartest firefighter in every situation.

Hero mode looks like:
- taking work back from the team to protect the deadline
- rewriting plans alone instead of improving planning quality
- solving every dependency issue personally
- joining every technical decision and never exiting

Why this fails:
- it hides systemic problems
- it weakens team ownership
- it teaches people to wait for rescue
- it scales badly

Better alternatives:
- shorten feedback loops
- define clearer ownership
- add checkpoints earlier
- improve decision quality instead of absorbing decision volume
- intervene at the constraint, not at every symptom

### Accountability Without Micromanagement
The manager's question is not "Did I tell them exactly what to do?"
It is:
- did the person understand the outcome
- did they understand the constraints
- were checkpoints appropriate to the risk
- did I verify shared understanding early enough
- was the task delegated at the right level of maturity

### Delegation by Maturity
Use different delegation styles depending on the person's experience and reliability.

For low-maturity or low-context situations:
- define outcome
- define deadline
- define constraints
- define checkpoints
- review early

For high-maturity situations:
- define outcome
- align on tradeoffs
- let them choose approach
- review at major decision points, not constantly

### Diagnostic Questions for Leverage Problems
When stuck, ask:
- is this a clarity problem, a capability problem, an incentive problem, or an authority problem
- am I asking for a decision, resources, alignment, or permission
- what is the smallest reversible step
- what happens if we do nothing
- who needs to agree vs who only needs visibility

### Common Reframes
- from "I do not have authority" to "which lever is still available"
- from "the team is failing" to "where is the system breaking"
- from "I need leadership to help" to "I need a decision on tradeoff X"
- from "I should fix this myself" to "how do I prevent recurrence"

### Practical Exercise Prompt
Take one current problem and write four lines:
- problem
- what I control
- what I can influence
- what I need decided above my level

## Module 2 Notes: Communication and Alignment

### Objective
Learn how to create clarity across managers, stakeholders, peers, and reports so decisions are made early, expectations stay realistic, and surprises are rare.

### Core Idea
Communication is not mainly about being articulate. It is about reducing ambiguity, aligning expectations, and making tradeoffs visible at the right time.

### What Strong Manager Communication Does
- makes priorities explicit
- makes risk visible early
- creates shared understanding of tradeoffs
- prevents silent drift
- adapts the message to the audience
- turns vague concern into actionable decisions

### Main Directions of Communication

#### Upward
Used to advocate, escalate, frame tradeoffs, and maintain trust with your manager and leadership.

#### Sideways
Used to align with peers, partner teams, product, design, recruiting, and operations.

#### Downward
Used to create clarity for the team, explain context, set expectations, and maintain accountability.

### The Basic Communication Loop
For important work, repeat this cycle:
1. state the goal
2. state the current status
3. state the main risk or tradeoff
4. state the recommendation or ask
5. confirm understanding

### Audience Translation
The same problem should be communicated differently depending on who is listening.

Leadership version:
- business impact
- timing risk
- cost of delay
- recommendation

Team version:
- objective
- constraints
- quality bar
- decision logic

Peer-team version:
- dependency
- owner
- timeline
- what happens if something slips

### Advocacy to Management
Advocating upward is not "selling yourself" in a shallow sense. It means making the work, the risk, and the decision need legible to people above you.

Good advocacy includes:
- what matters
- what is working
- what is at risk
- what support is needed
- what decision you recommend

Bad advocacy includes:
- raw frustration
- only reporting status without meaning
- waiting until failure is obvious
- asking for help without a clear ask

### Stakeholder Communication Model
For any stakeholder group, clarify:
- what they care about
- what they need to know
- when they need to know it
- what decision or action you need from them

### Expectation Management
Trust is not mainly built by always delivering on the original date.
Trust is built by making changes visible early, with clear reasons and tradeoffs.

Useful rule:
Bad news gets cheaper the earlier it is communicated.

### Communication Failure Modes
- saying too little because you want to avoid drama
- saying too much without clear meaning
- reporting progress without naming risk
- naming risk without recommendation
- using the same language with every audience
- replacing clarity with diplomacy

### Conflict Styles by Company
Different companies tolerate different styles:
- direct and debate-heavy
- consensus-oriented
- manager-driven
- document-driven
- relationship-driven

To identify the local style, observe:
- where real decisions get made
- whether disagreement happens in meetings or before them
- who can openly challenge whom
- whether written docs carry more weight than live discussion
- how often leaders reward candor vs smoothness

### Adapting Without Losing Integrity
Do not mirror the culture blindly. Adapt your method, not your standards.

Examples:
- in a direct culture, be concise and evidence-based
- in a consensus culture, pre-align before the main meeting
- in a document-driven culture, write before discussing
- in a manager-driven culture, escalate with explicit options

### Simple Manager Communication Templates

#### Status Update
- goal
- current status
- risk
- next step
- ask, if any

#### Early Warning
- what changed
- impact
- options
- recommendation

#### Stakeholder Alignment
- what we are doing
- why
- what this means for you
- what decision or support we need

### Practical Exercise Prompt
For one real initiative, write:
- the message for leadership
- the message for the team
- the message for a peer stakeholder

## Module 3 Notes: Planning, Prioritization, and Delivery

### Objective
Learn how to turn deadlines, ambiguity, and pressure into a credible delivery plan instead of a panic-driven execution mode.

### Core Idea
The manager's job is not to force effort out of engineers. It is to create a realistic path to the most valuable outcome within the constraint that exists.

### What Delivery Management Actually Means
- clarify the real deadline constraint
- define the minimum valuable scope
- sequence work for learning and risk reduction
- expose dependency risk early
- make tradeoffs explicit
- maintain decision quality under pressure

### CEO Deadline Rule
When leadership says a date is fixed, the manager should not translate that into fear for the team.
The manager should translate it into a constrained planning problem:
- if date is fixed, scope must move
- if scope is fixed, date must move
- if both appear fixed, quality, staffing, or risk posture is being changed whether stated or not

### What Not to Do
- threaten the team
- convert urgency into vague pressure
- let work expand without explicit tradeoffs
- hide uncertainty from leadership
- assume effort alone can solve planning problems

### A Better Manager Response to a Hard Deadline
1. confirm what is truly fixed
2. define MVP or minimum acceptable outcome
3. identify risks, assumptions, and dependencies
4. break work into short, integrated milestones
5. track progress against decision points, not only effort
6. escalate tradeoffs early when reality changes

### Manager vs Senior IC Lens
Senior IC lens often focuses on:
- architecture quality
- decomposition
- implementation approach
- parallelization
- keeping branches short

Manager lens must additionally focus on:
- business value
- sequencing by risk
- cross-team dependencies
- staffing and ownership
- timeline credibility
- communication and expectation management
- fallback plans if assumptions fail

### Important Reframe
Your IC instincts are not wrong. They are incomplete.
Good managers still care about decomposition and iterative delivery, but they connect those choices to business value, risk, and organizational coordination.

### MVP Planning Rule
The first goal is not "build the full solution."
The first goal is "reach the earliest version that is useful, demonstrable, and can survive if later scope slips."

### Delivery Questions a Manager Should Ask
- what is the smallest valuable thing we can ship by the date
- what must be true for that to happen
- what can be deferred safely
- what dependencies can break this plan
- what risks need proof early
- where do we need checkpoints or kill criteria
- what is the fallback if one major assumption fails

### Decomposition Quality
Good decomposition should:
- create short feedback loops
- reduce hidden integration risk
- make progress visible
- avoid long-lived isolated branches
- allow partial value or partial proof to emerge early

### Parallelization Caveat
Parallelization is useful only if ownership, interfaces, and integration points are clear.
Otherwise parallel work creates false progress and late merge pain.

### Delivery Under Pressure
Under pressure, managers should increase:
- clarity
- visibility
- milestone precision
- risk review frequency

Managers should not mainly increase:
- emotional intensity
- hours by default
- random status pings without decisions attached

### Early Warning Principle
When a plan is at risk, bring leadership a choice before the miss, not an explanation after the miss.

### Practical Exercise Prompt
For one pressured deadline, write:
- fixed date
- minimum valuable scope
- biggest dependencies
- top three risks
- fallback if risk one materializes

### Accountability When a Slip Has a Clear Owner
If one person's miss materially contributed to the slip, the manager should not ignore that. But the manager should handle it in the right order.

Order:
1. stabilize the delivery situation
2. communicate impact and tradeoffs
3. diagnose individual contribution accurately
4. address accountability and performance directly

Important rule:
Root-cause the system and address the person.
Do not choose only one.

Questions to ask:
- was the expectation clear
- was the task matched to the person's maturity
- were checkpoints early enough
- did the person raise risk in time
- did they miss because of capability, ownership, judgment, or effort

### Personal Accountability vs Scapegoating
Healthy accountability means:
- naming the specific miss
- explaining the impact
- clarifying the expected behavior next time
- changing support or scope if needed

Scapegoating means:
- collapsing a complex miss into one villain
- skipping process failures because a person failed visibly
- using blame as a substitute for diagnosis

### When the Date Is Truly Impossible
If the date remains impossible even after scope reduction, the manager's job is to surface impossibility early and turn it into an executive decision.

The wrong move:
- hide it
- hope effort closes the gap
- pressure the team into magical thinking

The right move:
- state that the current target is not achievable under present constraints
- explain why
- present constrained options
- recommend one

### Hard Truth Rule
Managers are not paid to promise the impossible convincingly.
They are paid to make reality legible in time for the company to respond intelligently.

### Delivery Framework for Hard Deadlines

#### 1. Diagnose the Deadline
- why is the date fixed
- what exactly must be true on that date
- what flexibility exists in rollout, audience, geography, feature flags, manual steps, or readiness criteria

#### 2. Define Minimum Valuable Scope
- what is the smallest thing that still matters
- what can be cut
- what can be delayed
- what can be manual or internal-first

#### 3. Map Risks and Dependencies
- top technical risks
- top cross-team risks
- top decision dependencies
- what assumption must be proven early

#### 4. Decompose Into Short Integrated Milestones
- thin vertical slices
- frequent integration
- short-lived branches
- visible progress checkpoints

#### 5. Set Confidence Checkpoints
At predefined points ask:
- are we still on path
- what has changed
- what tradeoff is now required
- do we cut scope, change rollout, or escalate

#### 6. Prepare Fallback Plans
For each major risk, define:
- what gets cut
- what becomes manual
- what shifts post-launch
- what launch shape remains possible

#### 7. Communicate on a Cadence
Leadership:
- status
- confidence
- risks
- decisions needed

Team:
- priorities
- changes in scope
- current blockers
- definition of success

Peer stakeholders:
- dependencies
- owner
- due date
- consequence of slip

#### 8. Escalate Before Failure, Not After
Bring options before the plan breaks completely.

#### 9. After Outcome, Review Honestly
- what was a planning error
- what was an execution error
- what was a dependency error
- what was an individual performance issue
- what system change is needed

## Module 4 Notes: Team Execution and Delegation

### Objective
Learn how to keep work moving through the team without becoming the bottleneck, the rescuer, or the person who silently absorbs every risk.

### Core Idea
The manager's execution job is to improve flow and decision quality, not to personally carry the hardest work forever.

### Three Main Manager Responsibilities in Execution
- keep priorities clear
- remove or surface blockers early
- match task scope and autonomy to the person's maturity and the risk level

### How to Help a Team Get Unstuck
When work stalls, diagnose the real type of blockage:
- clarity problem
- skill problem
- decision problem
- dependency problem
- priority conflict
- motivation or ownership problem

Different causes need different interventions. Do not use the same response for all stalls.

### Unsticking Playbook
1. identify the concrete point of stall
2. ask what is unknown, blocked, or contested
3. decide whether the issue needs clarification, coaching, escalation, or replanning
4. shorten the loop until signal improves
5. fix the recurring cause, not only the current symptom

### Delegation Rule
Delegation is not just assigning work.
Delegation is transferring ownership with the right level of clarity, support, and inspection.

### What Good Delegation Includes
- expected outcome
- why it matters
- deadline or decision point
- constraints and non-negotiables
- stakeholders involved
- checkpoint cadence
- what to do if risk appears

### Delegation by Risk and Maturity
For high-risk work or unproven people:
- tighter scope
- earlier checkpoints
- more explicit success criteria
- more active support

For lower-risk work or proven people:
- broader ownership
- less prescriptive execution
- review at major decision points

### If Delegated Work Slips
Do not jump straight to blame or rescue.
Ask:
- was the outcome clear
- were checkpoints early enough
- was the task scoped correctly
- did the person surface risk in time
- is this a one-off or a pattern

Then choose a response:
- clarify
- re-scope
- coach
- increase inspection
- reassign
- treat as a performance issue if repeated

### No Safe Buffer Problem
If there is no safe budget for failure, that does not mean "do not delegate anything."
It means:
- delegate smaller slices
- create earlier checkpoints
- keep high-risk decisions closer to senior people
- avoid all-or-nothing handoffs

### Manager Trap
Many managers say they cannot delegate because there is no room for failure.
Often the real issue is they are delegating too large a unit with too little visibility.

### Developer Experience
Developer experience is not a vanity topic.
It directly affects speed, quality, morale, and predictability.

Look for:
- slow feedback loops
- unclear ownership
- painful local setup
- unstable environments
- noisy review process
- flaky tests
- excessive waiting on approvals
- poor documentation around common tasks

### Framework to Find Team Usability Issues
Treat the team's workflow like a product.
Ask:
- where do people get confused
- where do they wait
- where do they redo work
- where do they need tribal knowledge
- where do handoffs fail
- where does progress become invisible

### Practical Rule
If the same kind of blockage appears three times, it is no longer an individual issue only. It is now a system issue.

### Manager Upgrade
Senior IC tendency:
- solve the hard problem yourself
- unblock by personal intervention
- improve local technical quality

Manager tendency:
- improve team flow
- make bottlenecks visible
- build better ownership patterns
- reduce repeat stalls

### Practical Exercise Prompt
Take one recurring team blockage and write:
- symptom
- likely root cause
- one immediate fix
- one system fix

## Module 5 Notes: 1:1s, Feedback, and Coaching

### Objective
Learn how to use regular manager conversations to improve clarity, trust, performance, growth, and retention rather than treating 1:1s as status meetings.

### Core Idea
1:1s are not mainly for updates. They are the manager's primary tool for understanding reality early, improving judgment, and helping individuals perform and grow.

### What 1:1s Are For
- understanding what is really going on
- surfacing concerns before they become incidents
- giving feedback
- coaching judgment and ownership
- clarifying expectations
- supporting growth and motivation

### What 1:1s Are Not For
- replacing project status tracking
- reading task updates that belong in tools
- vague morale theater
- only talking when something is wrong

### Basic 1:1 Structure
1. check context and energy
2. discuss the most important current issues
3. give or receive feedback
4. talk about growth, patterns, or longer-term development
5. confirm actions or follow-ups

### Three Lenses for a Good 1:1
- delivery: what is helping or hurting execution
- relationship: trust, communication, friction, motivation
- development: growth, skills, stretch, long-term direction

### Manager Question Types
Use questions that reveal signal, not perform politeness.

Examples:
- what feels unclear right now
- where are you blocked but have not escalated yet
- what are you worried we are underestimating
- what has been frustrating repeatedly
- where do you want more ownership
- what feedback do you have for me

### Feedback Rule
Feedback should be:
- specific
- timely
- behavior-based
- tied to impact
- clear about what should continue or change

### Simple Feedback Formula
- here is what I observed
- here is the impact
- here is what I want to see more of or differently

### How to Frame "I Want to See More Of or Differently"
The risk is not the phrase itself. The risk is surprise, vagueness, or making the person feel judged as a person instead of coached on behavior.

Use this sequence:
1. state supportive intent
2. name the pattern with one or two examples
3. explain the impact
4. state the expected behavior change
5. invite reaction and discuss support

Example:
"I want to share something that I think will help you be more effective here. On X and Y, I noticed blockers were surfaced very late. That made planning harder and reduced our options. Going forward, I want to see risks raised earlier, even if the full solution is not clear yet. How does that read from your side?"

### Good Framing Principles
- no surprise if possible
- behavior, not identity
- examples, not general impressions
- future-focused, not only retrospective
- explicit support, not only demand

### Bad Framing Patterns
- "You need to be more senior"
- "You seem disengaged"
- "People are worried about you"
- "I expected better"

### Better Alternatives
- "I want to see more proactive risk communication"
- "I want to see stronger ownership of follow-through"
- "I want to see earlier alignment when priorities are unclear"
- "I want to see more concise stakeholder updates on this workstream"

### Additional 1:1 Notes

#### How to Structure 1:1s Week to Week
Keep a light recurring shape, but do not make it rigid.

Useful weekly pattern:
- quick personal and context check
- most important current issue
- feedback or pattern discussion
- growth, motivation, or longer-term topic
- follow-ups and commitments

Not every week needs equal depth in every area, but over time all areas should get attention.

#### How to Keep 1:1s From Becoming Status Meetings
Do not use the meeting to read back ticket updates that already exist in tools.

If status starts taking over, shift with questions like:
- what is the real concern underneath this update
- where do you need support or clarity
- what risk are you worried about
- what should we discuss here that would not show up in Jira or Slack

#### How to Give Difficult Feedback Live
When the topic is sensitive:
- lower the drama
- increase specificity
- slow down your pace
- leave room for response

Simple live structure:
1. I want to discuss something important and useful
2. here is what I observed
3. here is why it matters
4. here is what I need to see differently
5. how does this look from your side
6. agree on next steps

#### Emotional Rule
Do not make feedback emotionally heavier than the facts require.
Calm, direct, and specific is stronger than intense or overly apologetic.

### Supportive but Clear Feedback
Good feedback does not choose between kindness and directness.
It combines:
- respect
- concrete examples
- explicit expectation
- belief that improvement is possible

### Coaching vs Telling
Tell when:
- risk is high
- time is short
- the person lacks key context

Coach when:
- the person can reason through it
- the goal is judgment growth
- the mistake cost is manageable

### Common 1:1 Failure Modes
- turning every 1:1 into project status
- avoiding hard feedback until it becomes severe
- overfocusing on immediate delivery and ignoring development
- making the conversation all about the manager's agenda
- failing to follow up on repeated concerns

### Notes and Documentation
Managers should keep light records of:
- commitments
- feedback themes
- growth goals
- repeated concerns
- notable wins or misses

This is not bureaucratic overkill.
It helps avoid:
- recency bias
- vague performance judgments
- forgotten commitments
- unfair evaluations

### Example 1:1 Topics
- ownership gap on a recent project
- conflict with a teammate
- desire for promotion path clarity
- repeated frustration with reviews or planning
- stretch opportunity for next quarter

### Practical Exercise Prompt
For one direct report, write:
- one execution topic to discuss
- one feedback topic
- one growth topic
- one question you should ask and not just tell

## Module 6 Notes: Performance, Conflict, and Inherited Teams

### Objective
Learn how to handle weak performance, interpersonal friction, and inherited team problems without becoming avoidant, unfair, or overly reactive.

### Core Idea
The manager's job is not to keep everyone comfortable. It is to create a fair, high-functioning environment where expectations are clear, problems are addressed early, and the team can trust that standards are real.

### Performance Management Basics
Performance management starts before formal HR process.
It begins with:
- clear expectations
- observed evidence
- direct feedback
- support and calibration
- follow-through

### Common Performance Failure Modes From Managers
- vague expectations
- waiting too long to address a pattern
- over-indexing on intent or likability
- skipping documentation
- jumping to formal process too early without clear feedback first
- staying in indefinite "wait and see" mode

### Diagnosing a Performance Problem
Ask:
- is this a capability issue
- is this an execution or ownership issue
- is this a motivation issue
- is this a clarity or environment issue
- is this a one-time miss or a repeated pattern

### A Fair Performance Sequence
1. define the gap in observable terms
2. discuss it directly
3. clarify the expected behavior or output
4. provide support or recalibration if appropriate
5. observe over a defined period
6. escalate formally if the gap persists

### Low Performer Rule
Do not leave someone in prolonged ambiguity.
If the person is below expectations, they should know that clearly and early.

### Conflict Management
Conflict is not automatically bad.
Unmanaged conflict is bad.

Useful conflict categories:
- task conflict
- role or ownership conflict
- style conflict
- trust conflict
- values or behavior conflict

The response depends on the category. Do not treat all tension as a personality issue.

### Conflict Response Pattern
1. identify the real source of friction
2. separate facts from interpretation
3. clarify the shared goal
4. name the behavior or decision problem
5. agree on operating changes or decision rules
6. follow up to see if the pattern improved

### Arrogant or High-Ego Developer
Do not make this about personality labels.
Translate it into observable impact:
- dismisses input
- creates fear in meetings
- blocks decision flow
- refuses shared standards
- damages collaboration

Then manage the impact directly.

### Inherited Team Rule
When you inherit a team, you do not inherit zero accountability.
You become accountable for what happens next, not guilty for how the team was formed before you arrived.

### First Steps With an Inherited Team
- observe patterns before forcing major change
- identify the real standards currently in operation
- understand existing trust dynamics
- find who is influential, not only who is senior by title
- clarify expectations and operating norms early

### Underperforming Existing Team
If the whole team underdelivers:
- do not individualize the problem too quickly
- inspect planning quality, ownership clarity, dependency handling, and standards
- then identify where specific people are also underperforming

### Ownership as a New Manager
You can say:
- "I did not create this situation, but I am responsible for improving it now."

That is the right posture:
- not defensive
- not guilty
- not passive

### Practical Exercise Prompt
For one team problem, write:
- what pattern you observe
- what might be system-level
- what might be individual-level
- what conversation should happen first

## Module 7 Notes: Hiring, Team Design, and Onboarding

### Objective
Learn how to shape a team over time through hiring, role definition, onboarding, and team-structure decisions rather than treating hiring as a one-off recruiting event.

### Core Idea
Hiring is not just filling seats. It is shaping the team's future execution capacity, standards, and culture. Onboarding is part of hiring quality, not an afterthought.

### What the Manager Owns in Hiring
- defining what problem the role is meant to solve
- clarifying level and expectations
- partnering with recruiting or HR
- assessing candidates against real team needs
- making tradeoffs explicit
- closing candidates
- making the hire successful after they join

### What HR or Recruiting Often Owns
- process coordination
- sourcing support
- compensation process
- legal and policy handling
- scheduling and operational flow

### Important Principle
Even when HR owns parts of the process, the manager still owns hiring quality.

### Before Hiring
Ask:
- what problem are we solving by adding this person
- is this a skill gap, capacity gap, or ownership gap
- what level do we really need
- what good looks like in the first 3 to 6 months

### Sourcing and Networks
Using LinkedIn or your network is fine.
The manager's mistake is not sourcing. The mistake is treating familiarity as evidence of fit.

Use networks to widen funnel, not to skip evaluation.

### Interviewing Rule
Interview for the real work, not for abstract brilliance.

Look for:
- problem-solving quality
- ownership behavior
- communication
- judgment under ambiguity
- ability to operate at the level needed on your team

### Hiring Tradeoff
A great interview signal is not enough.
You must ask:
- will this person succeed in this environment
- do they match the level we need
- can we support them effectively

### Team Design
Team design is about more than headcount.
It includes:
- ownership boundaries
- role clarity
- seniority mix
- dependency structure
- how work is grouped across people

### Onboarding Rule
Onboarding is where the company and manager prove that their expectations are real and their support is competent.

### Manager Onboarding of New Hires
The manager should make sure a new person gets:
- clear role expectations
- context on priorities and stakeholders
- first useful tasks that are meaningful but bounded
- known support contacts
- early checkpoints
- clarity on how success will be judged

### New Hire Onboarding Timeframes
First day:
- orientation
- role framing
- immediate contacts
- first practical next step

First week:
- team context
- systems access
- first small wins
- expectations for communication and work style

First month:
- growing ownership
- clearer performance calibration
- stakeholder map
- early feedback loops

### Your Own Onboarding as a Manager
When you join a company as a new manager, your first job is not to prove yourself through immediate change.
Your first job is to build context, trust, and map the system accurately.

### First Day as a New Manager
- show up prepared and calm
- meet immediate manager and team
- understand current priorities
- avoid overpromising

### First Week as a New Manager
- learn the business context
- meet key stakeholders
- understand team history, norms, and pain points
- ask more than you declare

### First Month as a New Manager
- identify patterns and risks
- clarify expectations and operating norms
- start making targeted improvements
- build trust through consistency, not theater

### Laying Roots at a Company
A better phrase is usually "building trust and influence."

This comes from:
- reliability
- thoughtful listening
- good follow-through
- understanding the org's real decision paths
- helping without pretending to know everything too early

### Common Onboarding Mistakes
- changing too much too fast
- staying passive too long
- failing to clarify expectations
- giving new hires work without context
- assuming a senior hire needs no onboarding

### Practical Exercise Prompt
Write:
- what a new hire should know on day 1, week 1, and month 1
- what you should learn on day 1, week 1, and month 1 as a new manager

## Module 8 Notes: Metrics and Management Operating System

### Objective
Learn how to measure team health and delivery quality without turning metrics into vanity dashboards, fear tools, or false precision.

### Core Idea
Metrics are useful when they improve decisions.
They are harmful when they replace judgment, create gaming, or hide context.

### What Metrics Are For
- noticing patterns early
- checking whether interventions are working
- making team health and delivery quality more visible
- improving decision quality

### What Metrics Are Not For
- proving that management is scientific
- ranking people simplistically
- replacing direct observation
- pressuring teams through numbers alone

### Important Principle
No single metric should carry too much meaning.
Use a small set of indicators and interpret them with context.

### Main Metric Categories

#### Delivery Metrics
- predictability
- cycle time
- throughput
- spillover rate
- milestone hit rate

#### Quality Metrics
- defect rate
- incident frequency
- escaped bugs
- rework rate
- rollback or recovery patterns

#### Team Health Metrics
- attrition risk
- engagement signals
- ownership clarity
- repeated blockers
- cross-team friction

#### Operational Metrics
- review turnaround time
- flaky test rate
- environment stability
- setup friction
- deployment frequency or release friction

### Leading vs Lagging Indicators
Leading indicators tell you something early:
- rising spillover
- longer review times
- repeated dependency waits
- increasing blocker frequency

Lagging indicators tell you something after damage:
- missed launch
- attrition
- major incident
- missed quarter target

Managers should pay special attention to leading indicators.

### Metrics Interpretation Rule
Metrics should start conversations, not end them.

Example:
If cycle time rises, do not conclude "engineers are slow."
Ask:
- is work larger
- are reviews slower
- are dependencies increasing
- is quality debt creating rework

### Metrics and People Management
Be careful using metrics at the individual level.
Many engineering metrics are better for diagnosing system health than ranking people.

Useful rule:
Use metrics to find where to look.
Use observation, feedback, and evidence to judge people fairly.

### Small Useful Manager Dashboard
If you had to keep it light, track:
- delivery confidence vs commitments
- spillover trend
- top recurring blockers
- review or integration bottlenecks
- defects or incidents trend
- notable team health concerns

### Weekly Management Operating System
A manager benefits from a repeatable weekly cadence.

Useful weekly rhythm:
- review team priorities and risks
- check progress on committed work
- look for slips or bottlenecks
- run 1:1s
- align with stakeholders
- capture important people notes
- review whether any escalation is needed

### Anti-Patterns
- too many metrics
- metrics with unclear actionability
- dashboard theater
- measuring only output volume
- rewarding local speed while harming overall quality
- using metrics as surprise ammunition in performance discussions

### Practical Exercise Prompt
Write:
- three leading indicators you would watch
- two lagging indicators you would track
- one metric you would avoid overusing

### If Management Is Not Scientific, What Is It
Management is a judgment discipline.
It uses evidence, but it also requires interpretation, tradeoffs, timing, incentives, and human understanding under uncertainty.

Useful framing:
- not pure science
- not pure art
- applied judgment in a social and operational system

### If Metrics Do Not End Conversations, What Does
Conversations should usually end with:
- a decision
- a tradeoff accepted explicitly
- an owner
- a next checkpoint

Metrics inform those outcomes. They do not replace them.

### Predictability
Predictability is usually measured as the gap between what the team said it would deliver and what it actually delivered.

Simple signals:
- commitment vs completion ratio
- milestone hit rate
- spillover trend
- forecast accuracy over repeated planning cycles

### Attrition Risk
Attrition risk is rarely measured by one number.
It is inferred from a pattern of signals, such as:
- repeated frustration in 1:1s
- compensation dissatisfaction
- stalled growth or promotion path
- persistent overload
- withdrawal from team discussion
- loss of ownership energy
- strong external marketability combined with internal dissatisfaction

### Repeated Dependency Wait
If another team repeatedly delays you, treat it as a management problem, not just an engineering annoyance.

Steps:
1. quantify the delay pattern
2. name the impact on your delivery
3. align directly with the peer owner
4. clarify owner, due date, and escalation path
5. reduce dependence where possible
6. escalate if the pattern persists and affects business commitments
